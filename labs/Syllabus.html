Day 1 — Week 1 — Intro

Welcome to AI: Where It Comes From & Where It’s Going

Learning Objectives

  • Articulate what AI is and name 3+ real-world examples they interact with daily
  • Distinguish between supervised ML, computer vision, and generative AI
  • Preview all four modules and understand how the two weeks connect
  • Run a Jupyter K-12 notebook and interact with all three types of AI hands-on

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
Instructor Intro (10 min) “Who am I?” — instructor background and motivation. Sets the tone for the two weeks. 0:00–0:10 Warm and personal
What is AI? (15 min) Slides: definition (learning from examples vs. following rules), why AI is having its moment now (data + compute + algorithms), and what AI is NOT (not magic, not conscious, not always right, not one thing). 0:10–0:25 Dispel myths early
“Does It Use AI?” (15 min) Class vote on 7 tech examples (AI Chatbot, Fitness Tracker, Calculator, Washing Machine, Music App, Phone Camera, Video Game) — show of hands or Mentimeter. Instructor reveals and explains each. 0:25–0:40 Encourage debate on “maybe” cases
AI All Around You (10 min) Students add their own examples to a Miro board with three pre-labeled zones: Supervised ML / Computer Vision / Generative AI. Reinforces the classification before the deep-dive. 0:40–0:50 Miro board set up in advance
Types of AI (20 min) Detailed slides: supervised ML (regression and classification with forward references to Days 2–5), computer vision (pixels → CNNs → applications), generative AI (LLMs and diffusion models). Ends with the two-week journey map. 0:50–1:10 10 slides; keep pace brisk
Introducing Notebooks (10 min) What a notebook is, brief history of Jupyter, anatomy of a notebook cell, intro to Jupyter-K12. 1:10–1:20 4 slides
Hello World + API Key Setup (20 min) Instructor demos the Hello World notebook live, then students open it and run each cell themselves. While students finish, distribute and enter OpenRouter API keys — students paste their key into the platform environment settings. 1:20–1:40 Pair struggling students
AI in Action Notebook (15 min) Students open the AI in Action notebook and work through three sections: spam detector (Supervised ML), live webcam object detection (Computer Vision), and generative Q&A with style control (Generative AI). Each section ends with a short challenge. Notebook CFU questions wrap up. 1:40–1:55 Part 3 requires API key from previous step
Wrap-Up & Preview (5 min) Instructor highlights one surprising moment from the room. Preview: tomorrow we dig into a real Mongolian air quality dataset. 1:55–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • Miro board with three pre-labeled zones (Supervised ML / Computer Vision / Generative AI)
  • Mentimeter or show-of-hands for “Does It Use AI?” votes
  • Jupyter-K12 platform
  • Hello World notebook (labs/01/notebooks/hello_world.ipynb)
  • AI in Action notebook (labs/01/notebooks/ai_in_action.ipynb)
  • OpenRouter API keys (one per student, distributed before class)

Day 2 — Week 1 — Module 1: Working with Data

The Air Mystery: What Impacts Ulaanbaatar’s Air the Most?

Learning Objectives

  • Open a Colab notebook, run cells, and load a CSV with pandas
  • Use .head(), .describe(), .value_counts(), and filtering to investigate a real question
  • Produce at least one meaningful matplotlib visualization that supports a finding
  • Explain why data quality and selection matter for AI — using today’s dataset as evidence

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
The Hook: A Mystery (10 min) Instructor opens with a single striking fact: in January, Ulaanbaatar regularly records PM2.5 levels 30–40× the WHO safe limit — worse than Beijing, worse than Delhi. Show a before/after satellite photo of the city in summer vs. winter smog. ‘Your job today: figure out why, using data.’ Introduce the detective framing: the dataset is your crime scene. 0:00–0:10 Show satellite comparison image
Setup & Tools (15 min) Walk through Google Colab. Every student runs: print('Investigation begins!'). Quick pandas demo: DataFrames, .head(), .info(), .describe() — framed as ‘reading the crime scene.’ 0:10–0:25 Pair struggling students
Investigation Sprint (40 min) Groups of 3 dig into the Ulaanbaatar AQI dataset with guided question cards (not open-ended — each card is a specific clue to chase). E.g., ‘Which month has the worst PM2.5? Which has the best?’, ‘Is there a pattern by day of week?’, ‘Does temperature predict pollution level?’ Groups record findings in a shared doc. 0:25–1:05 Question cards pre-prepared
Visualize Your Evidence (25 min) Each group turns their strongest finding into one chart using matplotlib templates. Frame: ‘A detective doesn’t just tell the jury — they show the evidence.’ 1:05–1:30 Templates in notebook
AI Training Data Lens (20 min) Pivot: ‘Now imagine you’re an AI company and this is your training data for a pollution prediction model. Would it be good enough? What’s missing? What would your model get wrong?’ Small group discussion, then share out. 1:30–1:50 Connects directly to Week 2
Wrap-Up (10 min) Each group posts their one-sentence finding to a class board. Preview: tomorrow you get an AI assistant to help with a harder dataset — and you’ll decide if it’s trustworthy. 1:50–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • Google Colab (browser, no install)
  • Ulaanbaatar historical AQI dataset (PM2.5, PM10, temperature, season — CSV, pre-cleaned from aqicn.org or IQAir historical download)
  • Starter notebook with scaffolded cells + matplotlib templates
  • Investigation question cards (printed, one set per group — 6–8 clues to chase)
  • Shared class findings doc (Google Doc or Jamboard)
  • Satellite comparison images: Ulaanbaatar summer vs. winter smog (freely available)

Day 3 — Week 1 — Module 1: Working with Data

AI as Co-Pilot: Can We Predict a Livestock Disaster?

Learning Objectives

  • Use a Claude-powered data assistant to query a dataset in plain English
  • Critically evaluate AI-generated interpretations against actual data
  • Train a simple linear regression model and interpret its predictions
  • Articulate the direct conceptual link between regression modeling and the micro:bit ML training in Module 2

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
The Hook: Dzud (10 min) Instructor introduces the dzud — Mongolia’s winter livestock disaster. Show the stat: in 2010, 20 million animals died in a single winter. In 2024, 7.4 million in just four months. ‘Herders knew a bad summer meant a bad winter. Can a machine learn that relationship from data?’ Introduce today’s dataset: aimag-level livestock mortality, summer drought index, and winter temperature, 1990–2013. 0:00–0:10 Map of Mongolia’s 21 aimags on screen
Introduce the AI Co-Pilot (15 min) Instructor demo: a Claude-powered chat interface loaded with the dzud dataset context. Ask it: ‘Which aimag suffered the most in 2010?’ Then ask something it gets wrong or oversimplifies. ‘Your job: use this tool as a starting point, then verify everything in code.’ 0:10–0:25 Claude.ai Project or Colab widget
Investigation: Human + AI (35 min) Groups explore the dataset with the AI co-pilot. Workflow: ask the assistant a question → verify in pandas → document where it’s right, wrong, or uncertain. Each group must catch the AI making at least 2 errors or oversimplifications before they present. 0:25–1:00 Encourage skepticism explicitly
Linear Regression: Predict the Disaster (25 min) Instructor-led walkthrough: train a linear regression model to predict livestock mortality from summer drought index and winter temperature. Students run it step-by-step. Key question: ‘This model sees the summer data — can it warn a herder before winter arrives?’ 1:00–1:25 Heavily scaffolded notebook
The Bridge Moment (5 min) Instructor draws the explicit connection: ‘Yesterday we looked for patterns in pollution data. Today we trained a model to predict disasters from patterns in climate data. On Thursday, you’ll do the exact same thing — but the data will be your own body movement, and the micro:bit will be the device that learns.’ Plant the seed. 1:25–1:30 Key conceptual moment — don’t rush
Data Story Presentations (20 min) Each group: 3 min presentation — the question, the evidence, one thing the AI got wrong, one limitation of the dataset. 1:30–1:50 Rubric: story, evidence, AI critique, limitation
Wrap-Up (10 min) Class vote: whose finding was most surprising? ‘What does it feel like to catch an AI being wrong?’ Preview: micro:bits arrive tomorrow. 1:50–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • Claude-powered data assistant (Claude.ai Project with dataset summary as context, or a lightweight Colab widget using the Anthropic API)
  • Dzud dataset: aimag-level livestock mortality + summer drought index + winter temperature, 1990–2013 (sourced from Mongolian NSO / Rao et al. 2015 supplementary data)
  • Colab notebook with linear regression walkthrough + visualization templates
  • Map of Mongolia’s 21 aimags (printed or on-screen)
  • Presentation rubric (story, evidence, AI critique, limitation — 1 page)
  • Optional: class leaderboard for ‘best AI catch of the day’

Day 4 — Week 1 — Module 2: Physical Computing

Computers in the Physical World: micro:bit & CreateAI

Learning Objectives

  • Connect a micro:bit to CreateAI and view live accelerometer data in real time
  • Train a gesture model with at least 3 classes and explain what ‘confidence threshold’ means
  • Understand that ML models learn from examples, not explicit rules
  • Write a MakeCode program that responds meaningfully to detected gestures

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
Hardware Orientation (15 min) Distribute micro:bits (groups of 2–3). Connect via USB, open createai.microbit.org. Instructor demo: accelerometer graph while shaking vs. still. 0:00–0:15 V2 required for on-device ML
First Model: Walk vs. Still (25 min) Guided: everyone trains the same model — ‘walking’ vs. ‘still’ vs. ‘other’. Collect 20 samples per class. Train and test on each other. 0:15–0:40 Use CreateAI guided tour
Iterate & Improve (20 min) Why does it fail on someone else’s wrist? Collect more diverse samples. Compare accuracy before/after. 0:40–1:00 Key insight: data quality matters
MakeCode Response (30 min) Export model to MakeCode. Program: walking → scroll message on LED; still → show symbol; other → show ‘?’. 1:00–1:30 Templates provided
Design Challenge (20 min) Groups sketch their own gesture-based device concept for tomorrow: what 3–4 gestures? What human need does it serve? 1:30–1:50 Design brief template
Wrap-Up (10 min) Discussion: How is this similar to scikit-learn? How is it different? Preview tomorrow’s build. 1:50–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • BBC micro:bit V2 (1 per 2–3 students) + USB cables + Chrome browser
  • createai.microbit.org + makecode.microbit.org
  • CreateAI getting started guide (microbit.org/createai)
  • Design brief template (1-pager)

Day 5 — Week 1 — Module 2: Physical Computing

Build Your Activity Detector: Physical AI Project

Learning Objectives

  • Design and train a multi-class gesture model for a purposeful application
  • Write a program that takes meaningful action on detected gestures
  • Debug and iterate on model quality (test on different people, orientations)
  • Articulate the human role in AI: labeling, choosing classes, and testing

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
Design Review (10 min) Groups share their sketch from Day 4. Quick peer feedback: is the human need clear? 0:00–0:10
Build & Train (60 min) Groups collect data, train, test, iterate. Prompt: ‘Does it still work on someone else’s arm? In a different room?’ Aim for 2–3 iteration cycles. 0:10–1:10 Iterate deliberately
Code the Response (35 min) Write the MakeCode or Python program. Micro:bit responds with LED, sound, or serial output. Push toward a complete, working demo. 1:10–1:45
Demo Gallery (15 min) Informal 2-min demo per group. Peers act as testers. Celebrate creative use of gestures. 1:45–2:00 Fun, low-stakes

Resources & Tools

  • micro:bits from Day 4
  • createai.microbit.org + MakeCode
  • Optional: buzzer accessories for audio feedback

Instructor Notes: Module 2 capstone. Accuracy matters less than purposeful design — celebrate a 70% accurate exercise counter more than a 95% accurate wave detector with no clear use. Great transition line: ‘Next week we’ll teach computers to see, not just move.’

Day 6 — Week 2 — Module 3: Computer Vision

How Computers See: CV Foundations + Mini-Project

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how a CNN classifies images at a conceptual level (pixels → features → predictions)
  • Run pre-trained classification, pose detection, and object detection models in the browser
  • Build a working CV application combining a pre-trained model with custom Python logic
  • Identify at least one ethical concern in computer vision technology

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
Hook (10 min) Show 10 images pixelated at varying levels. Students classify. ‘Computers start from pixels just like this bottom row.’ 0:00–0:10
CV Concepts (20 min) Instructor overview: pixels → CNN layers → predictions. Demo MobileNet on webcam in browser — confidence scores live. Show 3 task types: classification, pose detection, object detection. 0:10–0:30 TF.js demos, no GPU
Exploration Stations (25 min) Sequential or parallel: (1) Image classification with MobileNet, (2) Pose detection with MoveNet, (3) Object detection with COCO-SSD. Students screenshot one result from each. 0:30–0:55 Pre-built browser demos
Ethics Discussion (15 min) Case study: CV in surveillance, facial recognition bias. Small groups: ‘Should CV be used to monitor students in school?’ 2-min debrief. 0:55–1:10 Socratic format
Mini-Project Build (40 min) Groups choose a project track: (A) Pose Counter — count reps when keypoint crosses threshold. (B) Object Zone Game — detect object entering a screen zone. (C) Virtual Background — segmentation + canvas overlay. (D) Expression Mirror — MediaPipe Face Mesh reactions. 1:10–1:50 Template notebooks per track
Wrap-Up (10 min) Quick share: one unexpected CV challenge you hit. Preview: tomorrow we add language to vision. 1:50–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • TensorFlow.js browser demos (tensorflow.org/js/demos)
  • MediaPipe (mediapipe.dev) for pose/face
  • Project template notebooks (one per track)
  • Colab with webcam access (JS bridge) or pre-recorded video fallback

Day 7 — Week 2 — Module 3: CV → Module 4: Generative AI

Vision Language Models + How Generative AI Works

Learning Objectives

  • Describe what a VLM does and how it differs from a classifier
  • Explain tokenization with a hands-on example
  • Describe word embeddings as ‘coordinates for meaning’ in vector space
  • Explain the transformer architecture at a high level and connect it to VLMs

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
VLM Demo & Discussion (20 min) Instructor shows a VLM describing images — impressive case, then a failure case. ‘How is this different from MobileNet?’ Discussion: it produces language, not just a label. Show the two-component model: visual encoder + language model. 0:00–0:20 Claude API, GPT-4V, or HuggingFace Spaces (free)
VLM Exploration (25 min) Students query a VLM with their CV project screenshots + Mongolia/Naadam photos. Task: find one impressive result and one failure. Share findings. 0:20–0:45 No API key needed via HuggingFace
Bridge: From Vision to Language (5 min) Quick pivot: ‘VLMs describe images. What if AI could generate language from scratch — or generate images?’ Introduce Module 4. 0:45–0:50
Tokenization Activity (20 min) Students use platform.openai.com/tokenizer on Mongolian words, code, emoji, and their own name. Questions: how many tokens? Why does it matter for cost and context? 0:50–1:10 Physical token cards as backup
Embeddings Demo (20 min) Word2vec/semantic similarity: ‘King − Man + Woman ≈ Queen.’ Students try analogies in a Colab notebook. Instructor explains: these are the coordinates transformers learn from data. 1:10–1:30 Colab + gensim
Transformer Overview (20 min) Visual walkthrough of attention using ‘The bank by the river’ example. No math — diagrams only. Connect: ‘The LLM is doing this for every token, millions of times, trained on the same kind of data you used in Week 1.’ 1:30–1:50 Jay Alammar visuals
Wrap-Up (10 min) Exit ticket: draw the path from ‘user types a message’ → ‘LLM responds.’ Preview tomorrow’s chatbot build. 1:50–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • VLM access: HuggingFace Spaces (free LLaVA demo), Claude API, or GPT-4V
  • platform.openai.com/tokenizer (free, no login)
  • Colab notebook: gensim word embeddings
  • Jay Alammar ‘Illustrated Transformer’ diagrams (jalammar.github.io)

Day 8 — Week 2 — Module 4: Generative AI

Prompt Engineering & Building Your Chatbot

Learning Objectives

  • Apply 4+ prompt engineering techniques: persona, few-shot, chain-of-thought, constraints
  • Use Code.org’s AI Chat Lab to design and build a custom chatbot with a system prompt
  • Evaluate a chatbot’s responses critically and iterate on the design
  • Explain how system prompts shape model behavior

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
Prompt Warm-Up (15 min) Instructor shows the same question with 3 different prompts → very different outputs. Students guess which prompt produced which. Introduce: persona, few-shot, CoT, constraints. 0:00–0:15
Prompt Engineering Practice (25 min) Students experiment with a live LLM: explain photosynthesis to a 5-year-old vs. a PhD; add a constraint (‘no jargon’); try chain-of-thought for a logic problem. 0:15–0:40 Claude.ai or Code.org AI Chat Lab
Chatbot Design Intro (10 min) Introduce Code.org’s Exploring Generative AI curriculum and AI Chat Lab. Walk through system prompt anatomy. Show a pre-built ‘Cat Expert’ chatbot. 0:40–0:50 code.org — free, grades 8–12
Build Your Chatbot (60 min) Groups of 2 design and build a chatbot on a topic they care about. Requirements: clear persona, 3 topic focus areas, 1 ‘do not discuss’ boundary, tested with 5+ different questions. 0:50–1:50
Wrap-Up (10 min) Share chatbot name + one surprising thing it did. Preview: tomorrow you’ll add knowledge and show it off — plus explore image generation. 1:50–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • Code.org AI Chat Lab (code.org/en-US/curriculum/exploring-generative-artificial-intelligence)
  • Prompt engineering reference card (1-pager: 4 techniques with examples)
  • Example system prompts to study and remix
  • Claude.ai or similar LLM for free-form practice

Day 9 — Week 2 — Module 4: Generative AI

Chatbot Showcase + Image Generation

Learning Objectives

  • Deliver a polished chatbot demo including a system prompt, knowledge grounding, and graceful failure
  • Explain how diffusion models generate images at a conceptual level
  • Craft effective image generation prompts using a structured scaffold
  • Engage with IP and ethics questions around AI-generated art

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
RAG & Knowledge Grounding (15 min) Quick demo: same chatbot with and without a knowledge document. What changed? Why? Conceptual explanation of retrieval-augmented generation — no code required. 0:00–0:15
Polish Your Chatbot (25 min) Groups add a knowledge document (or expanded system prompt with facts) and evaluate using a peer rubric: helpfulness, accuracy, personality, graceful failure. 0:15–0:40 Printed rubric
Chatbot Showcase (20 min) Each group demos in 2 minutes: what it does, a successful exchange, something it won’t do. Peer votes: Most Creative, Most Useful, Best Personality. 0:40–1:00 Celebration atmosphere
Diffusion Model Concepts (15 min) How AI generates images: noise → denoise → image, latent space, text encoder. Visual only — no math. ‘This is the visual twin of what the LLM does with tokens.’ 1:00–1:15 Show denoising animation
Image Generation Workshop (30 min) Students generate images on Replicate (or Adobe Firefly / Canva AI). Prompt scaffold: [subject] + [style] + [lighting] + [mood]. Theme: inspired by Mongolia or Naadam. Iterate 3 times. 1:15–1:45 Replicate.com or free alternatives
Ethics: Art & AI (10 min) Quick discussion: Who owns AI-generated art? Should models be trained on artists’ work without consent? 2-min think-pair-share. 1:45–1:55
Preview Final Day (5 min) Explain tomorrow’s capstone structure. Remind groups to choose their showcase project. 1:55–2:00

Resources & Tools

  • Code.org AI Chat Lab
  • Peer chatbot evaluation rubric (4 dimensions, 1 page)
  • Replicate.com (hosted image models) or Adobe Firefly / Canva AI (free)
  • Diffusion explainer clip (Computerphile or 3Blue1Brown, ~5 min)
  • Prompt scaffold template (printable)

Day 10 — Week 2 — Capstone

AI Showcase: Putting It All Together

Learning Objectives

  • Synthesize learning across all four modules in a final project presentation
  • Demonstrate genuine understanding of at least one AI concept to peers
  • Articulate one societal implication of AI in a domain they care about
  • Leave feeling capable, curious, and inspired to keep building

Session Schedule (2 hours)

Segment Details Time Notes
Setup & Last-Mile Polish (20 min) Groups do final prep on their capstone project. Instructor confirms running order. 0:00–0:20
Final Project Presentations (60 min) Each group: 3–4 min presentation + 1 min Q&A. Project options: (A) Data Story — extended Module 1 investigation; (B) Physical AI Device — polished micro:bit project with docs; (C) CV Application — finished Module 3 project with demo; (D) Chatbot — polished bot with knowledge base; (E) Wild Card — cross-module project combining 2+ AI types. 0:20–1:20 All groups present
Letter to Myself (15 min) Each student writes: one AI application they want to build in the next year, and one thing they commit to thinking critically about. 1:20–1:35 Private or share optionally
Panel Reflection (10 min) Student-led open Q&A: What surprised you most? What do you wish AI could do that it can’t? What worries you? 1:35–1:45
Celebration & Certificates (15 min) Award recognition in all categories. Share ‘What’s Next’ resource card. Group photo. 1:45–2:00 Certificates printed in advance

Resources & Tools

  • Cross-module presentation rubric (technical accuracy, creativity, communication, ethics awareness)
  • Certificate template
  • “What to Explore Next” card: fast.ai · Kaggle Learn · CS50 AI · 3Blue1Brown · deeplearning.ai short courses
  • Optional: highlight reel compiled from Days 1–9
